# Current Research

My research focuses on the fundamental physics of particle transport in turbulent and wave-driven flows. 

I use a combination of laboratory experiments, particle-based measurements, and data-driven analysis to study these systems. These insights have direct implications for environmental and geophysical processes, including the transport of microplastics in the ocean, the dispersion of pollutants, the evolution of oil spills, and the dynamics of particles in the atmosphere.

## Current Work: Particle Transport in Free-Surface Flows
My doctoral research investigates how nearly neutrally buoyant particles are transported in a turbulent free-surface boundary layer. These flows are relevant to environmental systems such as oceans, rivers, and atmospheric interfaces.

I conduct controlled laboratory experiments in a wind-wave flume and analyze particle motion using particle tracking and particle image velocimetry (PIV). A central focus of this work is understanding how waves and turbulence affect the transport of near-neutrally buoyant particles.

## Wave–Turbulence Decomposition (Data-Driven Methods)
We developed a data-driven technique to separate wave motion from turbulent fluctuations in velocity fields. Using modal decomposition methods such as Dynamic Mode Decomposition (DMD), I identify coherent structures associated with waves and isolate turbulent contributions.

This approach enables more accurate quantification of transport processes in complex, multi-scale flows.

## Volumetric Flow Rate Measurement using remote Sensing Techniques
During my master’s research at The University of Texas at Austin, I worked with surface imaging techniques to estimate volumetric flow rates in open-channel flows. This work combined remote sensing, surface PIV, and turbulence theory to infer subsurface flow properties.

## Hydraulics and Energy Systems
As an undergraduate researcher, I worked on problems in river engineering and sustainable energy systems, including numerical modeling of flow around river structures and experimental development of siphon-based energy generation devices.

## Future Directions
I am interested in extending my work toward theoretical and computational frameworks for transport in turbulent and multiphase flows. In particular, I aim to develop reduced-order models and numerical simulations that complement experimental observations.
